# Cat Care Best Practices

## Exercise and Play Requirements

### Kittens (0-1 year)
- **Duration**: 30-60 minutes total, split into multiple sessions
- **Frequency**: 4-6 short play sessions daily (5-10 minutes each)
- **Type**: Interactive play with wand toys, laser pointers, feather toys
- **Peak times**: Dawn and dusk (natural hunting times)

### Adult Cats (1-7 years)
- **Duration**: 20-30 minutes daily, split into 2-3 sessions
- **Type**: Mix of interactive play and solo play
- **Indoor cats**: Need MORE play - no natural hunting outlet
- **Outdoor cats**: May self-exercise but still benefit from play

### Senior Cats (7+ years)
- **Duration**: 10-20 minutes daily
- **Type**: Gentler play, puzzle feeders, slow-moving toys
- **Considerations**: Arthritis may limit jumping - use ramps

## Feeding Guidelines

### Meal Frequency
- **Kittens (8-12 weeks)**: 4 meals daily
- **Kittens (3-6 months)**: 3 meals daily
- **Kittens (6-12 months)**: 2 meals daily
- **Adult cats**: 2 meals daily OR free-feeding (depends on cat)
- **Senior cats**: 2 meals daily, monitor weight

### Free-Feeding vs. Scheduled Feeding
- **Free-feeding**: Works for cats who self-regulate, single-cat homes
- **Scheduled feeding**: Better for multi-cat homes, overweight cats, medication needs
- **Wet food**: Must be scheduled (spoils at room temp after 30 min)

### Special Considerations
- **Diabetic cats**: MUST eat before insulin - exact timing critical
- **Kidney disease**: May need smaller, more frequent meals
- **Overweight cats**: Scheduled feeding with measured portions

## Medication Administration

### Time-Critical Medications
- **Insulin**: Given twice daily, exactly 12 hours apart, always with food
- **Thyroid medication**: Daily, ideally same time, with or without food
- **Antibiotics**: Complete full course, even if cat seems better

### Administration Tips
- Pill pockets or soft treats work well
- Liquid medications can be mixed with small amount of wet food
- Never skip diabetes medication - life-threatening
- For difficult cats, ask vet about compounded medications (flavored liquids)

## Litter Box Maintenance

### Frequency
- **Scooping**: At least twice daily (morning and evening)
- **Full clean**: Weekly (dump all litter, wash box, refill)
- **Multi-cat homes**: Scoop 3+ times daily

### Best Practices
- **Number of boxes**: Number of cats + 1 (e.g., 2 cats = 3 boxes)
- **Location**: Quiet, accessible, not near food/water
- **Type**: Most cats prefer unscented, clumping litter
- **Box size**: 1.5x cat's body length

### Health Monitoring
- Changes in litter box habits indicate health issues
- Straining to urinate = emergency (especially male cats)
- Diarrhea or blood in stool = vet visit needed

## Grooming Needs

### Brushing
- **Long-haired cats**: Daily brushing (prevents mats, hairballs)
- **Medium-haired cats**: 2-3 times weekly
- **Short-haired cats**: Weekly brushing
- **Shedding season**: Increase frequency for all coat types

### Nail Trimming
- **Frequency**: Every 2-3 weeks
- **Indoor cats**: More critical (no natural wear from outdoor surfaces)
- **Senior cats**: Nails grow faster, curve into paw pads if not trimmed

### Bathing
- **Standard**: Rarely needed - cats self-groom
- **Long-haired breeds**: Every 4-6 months if needed
- **Hairless breeds**: Weekly bathing required

### Dental Care
- **Brushing**: Daily ideal, 3x weekly minimum
- **Dental treats**: Daily as supplement (not replacement for brushing)
- **Professional cleaning**: As recommended by vet (often annually)

## Mental Enrichment

### Environmental Enrichment
- **Vertical space**: Cat trees, shelves, window perches (critical for indoor cats)
- **Hiding spots**: Boxes, tunnels, enclosed beds
- **Window access**: Bird watching, outdoor stimulation
- **Duration**: Available 24/7

### Interactive Enrichment
- **Puzzle feeders**: Daily use, especially for food-motivated cats
- **Play sessions**: 2-3 times daily, 10 minutes each
- **Rotation**: Swap toys weekly to maintain interest
- **DIY options**: Cardboard boxes, paper bags, treat hunts

### Cognitive Activities
- **Training**: Cats can learn tricks - 5-10 minute sessions
- **Clicker training**: Effective for mental stimulation
- **Foraging games**: Hide treats around home

## Health Monitoring

### Daily Observations
- Appetite and water intake
- Litter box usage (frequency, appearance)
- Energy levels and playfulness
- Vomiting frequency (occasional hairballs normal, frequent is not)

### Weekly Checks
- Weight (especially important for diabetic/kidney disease cats)
- Coat condition (mats, dander, oiliness)
- Eye and ear cleanliness

## Priority Scheduling

1. **Critical (Never Skip)**: Medications (insulin, thyroid, antibiotics)
2. **High Priority**: Feeding (especially if on medication), litter box scooping
3. **Important**: Play/exercise (critical for indoor cats), grooming (long-haired)
4. **Routine**: Environmental enrichment setup, nail trimming
5. **Flexible**: Training sessions, toy rotation

## Special Needs Considerations

### Diabetic Cats
- Insulin MUST be given at exact times (12 hours apart)
- Must eat before insulin - measure food, ensure cat eats
- Consistent exercise timing helps regulate glucose
- Monitor for hypoglycemia (lethargy, weakness)

### Indoor-Only Cats
- REQUIRE daily interactive play - no natural hunting outlet
- Need more environmental enrichment than outdoor cats
- Higher risk of obesity - monitor food intake carefully
- Mental stimulation critical to prevent behavioral issues

### Senior Cats (7+ years)
- May develop arthritis - provide ramps to favorite spots
- Kidney disease common - monitor water intake
- May need lower litter box sides
- More frequent vet visits for early disease detection

### Multi-Cat Households
- Separate feeding areas if food aggression present
- Multiple litter boxes (n+1 rule)
- Individual play time reduces stress
- Monitor for bullying/resource guarding

### Anxious Cats
- Consistent routine critical for reducing stress
- Vertical space provides escape routes
- Pheromone diffusers can help
- Enrichment activities reduce anxiety-related behaviors

## Behavioral Cues for Scheduling

### Signs Your Cat Needs More Play
- Destructive behavior (scratching furniture, knocking things over)
- Nighttime "zoomies" or excessive vocalization
- Aggression toward people or other pets
- Excessive grooming or overweight

### Signs Your Cat Needs Vet Visit (Urgent)
- Not eating for 24+ hours
- Straining to urinate or no urination
- Lethargy with no play interest
- Vomiting multiple times in 24 hours
- Difficulty breathing
